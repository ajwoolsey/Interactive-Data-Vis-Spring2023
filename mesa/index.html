Visualizing the Barriers to Park Access

Nature, considered the “great equalizer”1 to some, should act as a public, universal good. Living in proximity to a green space is associated with a wide range of mental and health benefits, including a reduced risk of type II diabetes, heart disease, and lower levels of stress hormones 2. In urban spaces, parks and green spaces play an especially vital role in maintaining ecological homeostasis, supporting climate resiliency and adaptation measures, promoting a connection to the environment, and fostering community engagement. Unfortunately, the benefits of natural areas in urban spaces are not equitably distributed across demographics in the United States.

The “nature gap” refers to the unequal distribution of natural spaces, specific to low-income communities and people of color. Due to a combination of systemic racism, redlining, and restrictive urban planning, there exists a massive discrepancy in green space and nature access, with marginalized communities largely living in areas deprived of quality parks and natural spaces. Overwhelmingly, neighborhoods of color and lower income communities have less access to parks and green spaces than white, affluent neighborhoods. In cities, neighborhoods with primarily non-white residents have less green space (calculated as acreage of park/person) than predominantly white neighborhoods3.

In order to close the park equity gap in cities, root causes of nature access discrepancies in urban spaces must be identified and investigated thoroughly.

This project seeks to address the issue of park access inequity in cities from a lens of structural racism in urban planning and “people-oriented city design”-transit development that services urban community needs over vehicle infrastructure 4. Non-profit Trust for the Public Land (TPL) has an indexed ranking of park access in American cities called “ParkScore”. ParkScore uses metrics including total park acreage, investment, amenities, access, and equity-sourced from US Census demographic data- to develop an aggregated park access ranking of America’s top 100 cities. In 2022, Washington, DC was designated the most equitable US city in terms of park access with a ParkScore of 84.9, with Mesa, AZ declared the least equitable with a score of 31.9 5. By comparing the most and the least park equitable cities in the United States, this project hopes to showcase park access inequalities as an issue of inequitable urban infrastructure.


Scatterplots

The scatterplots below provide situational context on the relationship between people-oriented city infrastructure and park access by comparing ParkScore rankings of 100 US Cities to metrics of walkability, bikeability, and access to transit. WalkScore, a subsidiary of the real estate company RedFin, measures the walkability, bikeability, and transit accessibility of any urban area, designated as Walk Score®, Bike Score®, and Transit Score®, respectively6. Data points for DC and Mesa are highlighted. Cities with higher walk, bike, and transit scores have more comprehensive people-oriented city infrastructure- including bike lanes, metro lines, and bus stations- than cities with lower scores. Overwhelmingly, there is a positive correlation between walkability, bikeability, transit access, and park equity, suggesting a relationship between people-oriented city infrastructure and park equity in the United States.
